Community Sanitation Programs

Building sustainable hygiene infrastructure through local engagement, education, and policy reform initiatives.

The Sanitation Challenge

Approximately 2 billion people worldwide lack access to basic sanitation facilities. Open defecation remains a critical public health issue in many regions, contributing to cholera, typhoid, and parasitic infections. SDG Target 6.2 aims to achieve access to adequate and equitable sanitation and hygiene for all, ending open defecation, with particular attention to the needs of women and girls.

Community-Led Total Sanitation (CLTS)

CLTS is a participatory approach that empowers communities to analyse their own sanitation conditions, leading to collective action without external subsidies. The methodology works through:

  • Triggering: Community mapping exercises that reveal the full extent of faecal contamination pathways.
  • Natural Leaders: Identifying and supporting motivated individuals who champion sanitation improvements.
  • Collective Decision-Making: Communities set their own timelines and standards for achieving open defecation free (ODF) status.
  • Monitoring: Peer accountability systems ensure sustained behaviour change beyond the initial intervention.

Impact of Better Sanitation

The WHO estimates that every $1 invested in sanitation yields a $5.50 return through reduced healthcare costs and increased productivity. Improved sanitation also has downstream effects on gender equality, as safe facilities reduce the vulnerability of women and girls to harassment. School attendance rates among girls increase by up to 15% when adequate sanitary facilities are available on campus.

Impact Statistic

Countries that have invested in CLTS programmes have seen a 30-50% reduction in diarrhoeal disease incidence within the first three years of implementation.
