Water Quality Monitoring

Scientific approaches to tracking, measuring, and ensuring safe water standards for communities worldwide.

Why Water Quality Matters

Water quality directly impacts human health, ecosystem stability, and economic productivity. Contaminated water is responsible for over 485,000 diarrhoeal deaths annually, according to the World Health Organisation. Effective monitoring systems are the first line of defence against waterborne diseases and environmental degradation.

Key Parameters Tracked

Modern water quality assessment involves testing for multiple parameters, each providing critical information about the safety and usability of water sources:

  • pH Level (6.5–8.5): Indicates acidity or alkalinity. Extreme pH levels can corrode infrastructure and harm aquatic life.
  • Turbidity: Measures suspended particles. High turbidity can harbour pathogens and reduce the effectiveness of UV disinfection.
  • Dissolved Oxygen (DO): Essential for aquatic ecosystems. Levels below 5 mg/L indicate poor water health.
  • Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD): Indicates organic pollution load. BOD exceeding 6 mg/L suggests significant contamination.
  • Heavy Metals: Lead, mercury, and arsenic are monitored at parts-per-billion thresholds due to their bioaccumulative toxicity.

Technology in Water Monitoring

The evolution of water monitoring technology has been transformative. Remote sensor arrays connected via IoT networks now provide continuous, real-time data from thousands of monitoring stations globally. Our Aquatic Integrity System (AIS) leverages machine learning algorithms to detect anomalies in water quality data, enabling proactive intervention before contamination events escalate. Satellite-based remote sensing offers macro-level analysis of surface water bodies, tracking eutrophication, algal blooms, and sedimentation patterns across entire watersheds.

Did You Know?

A single IoT water sensor can monitor water quality 24/7 for less than $0.05 per day in operational costs, making large-scale deployment economically viable even in developing nations.
